Tax Compliance for Small Businesses in Kenya

November 20, 2025 6 min read By Mary Wanjiku

Small businesses often struggle with complex tax obligations. This guide simplifies tax compliance in Kenya, covering income tax, VAT registration, payroll tax obligations, and the importance of maintaining proper records. We provide practical tips to minimize your tax burden legally and avoid penalties from the Kenya Revenue Authority.

For many entrepreneurs, navigating the maze of tax regulations can be one of the most challenging aspects of running a business. However, understanding and meeting your tax obligations is crucial for long-term success and avoiding legal trouble. The Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) has been actively working to simplify tax processes, but professional guidance is still invaluable.

Key Tax Obligations for Small Businesses

Here are some of the primary taxes that small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in Kenya need to be aware of:

  • Income Tax: This is a tax on the net profit of your business. For incorporated companies, this is charged at a standard rate of 30%. For sole proprietorships and partnerships, the income is taxed at the individual graduated rates.
  • Value Added Tax (VAT): If your business has a turnover of KES 5 million or more per year, you are required to register for VAT. You must then charge VAT at 16% on your taxable goods and services and remit it to the KRA.
  • Pay As You Earn (PAYE): If you have employees, you must deduct PAYE from their salaries and remit it to the KRA on their behalf. This also includes contributions to the National Hospital Insurance Fund (NHIF) and the National Social Security Fund (NSSF).
  • Turnover Tax (TOT): For small businesses with an annual turnover of between KES 1 million and KES 25 million, Turnover Tax is a simplified tax charged at 3% on the gross sales. This is an alternative to income tax.

Tips for Effective Tax Compliance

Staying on the right side of the KRA doesn't have to be a headache. Here are some practical tips:

  • Keep Accurate Records: Maintain meticulous records of all your income, expenses, and transactions. This is the foundation of good tax management.
  • Understand Deadlines: Be aware of all the filing and payment deadlines for different taxes to avoid late penalties.
  • Separate Business and Personal Finances: Open a separate bank account for your business to make tracking income and expenses easier.
  • Seek Professional Advice: A qualified tax advisor can help you structure your business in a tax-efficient way, identify eligible deductions, and ensure you are fully compliant.
